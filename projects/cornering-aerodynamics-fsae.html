Cornering Aerodynamics of a Formula SAE Race Car

Quantifying aerodynamic performance under straight-line and cornering flow conditions

Semester-long team project conducted with Carnegie Mellon Racing as part of a graduate CFD course.

Context

Formula SAE lap time is dominated by cornering performance, where available lateral grip limits vehicle speed more than straight-line acceleration. Aerodynamic downforce plays a critical role in this regime by increasing tire normal force and improving cornering capability.

Aerodynamic development is typically performed using straight-line CFD simulations due to their simpler domain setup and boundary conditions. While these simulations are effective for relative design comparison, they do not explicitly model the turning flow field experienced by the car during steady-state cornering.

This project evaluates how aerodynamic performance changes when the vehicle is subjected to a lateral velocity gradient representative of steady-state cornering. By comparing straight-line and cornering flow cases under consistent modeling assumptions, the study assesses how well conventional straight-line simulations capture cornering-relevant aerodynamic behavior.

Approach

The study was structured as a controlled comparison between straight-line and cornering flow conditions, with the goal of isolating the aerodynamic impact of turning flow while holding all other modeling choices constant.

A conventional straight-line CFD domain with uniform inlet velocity was used as the baseline case. Cornering was modeled using a curved, pie-shaped domain that imposed a lateral velocity gradient across the vehicle width, representing steady-state turning at a fixed radius.

Both configurations were evaluated across multiple vehicle speeds using identical turbulence modeling, meshing strategy, and solver settings. This ensured that observed differences in aerodynamic performance could be attributed to flow curvature rather than numerical or modeling artifacts.

Because absolute force comparisons are sensitive to velocity magnitude and domain differences, aerodynamic performance was assessed primarily using the downforce-to-drag ratio. This metric provides a robust basis for comparison across flow conditions and highlights changes in aerodynamic efficiency relevant to vehicle handling.

Implementation & Tools

Geometry and Domain Setup

  • Simplified but aerodynamically representative CAD model including front and rear wings, chassis and driver body, and wheels
  • Two computational domains: rectangular straight-line domain and pie-shaped cornering domain

CFD Solver and Physics

  • Solver: ANSYS Fluent (pressure-based coupled solver)
  • Flow model: Incompressible RANS
  • Turbulence model: k-omega SST
  • Discretization: Second-order spatial schemes
  • Boundary conditions: moving ground, rotating wheels, velocity inlet with imposed lateral gradient (cornering case)

Mesh Strategy and Compute

  • Watertight geometry meshing workflow
  • Poly-hexcore volume mesh
  • Boundary layer resolution with first cell height approx 0.5 mm and y+ approx 30
  • Mesh sizes ranging from 8 million to 21 million cells
  • Simulations run on the Bridges-2 HPC cluster

Analysis & Insights

Cornering flow reduces aerodynamic performance relative to straight-line cases, but the magnitude is modest. Downforce scales predictably with velocity, and the aerodynamic package remains effective relative to the no-aero configuration.

Drag shows higher sensitivity to flow curvature than downforce, contributing to the downforce-to-drag reduction. The consistent reduction across speeds indicates straight-line simulations remain useful for comparative design when their limits are acknowledged.

The cornering-flow CFD workflow is reusable for future aerodynamic iterations.

What This Demonstrates

  • Setup and execution of large-scale external aerodynamics CFD
  • Turbulence modeling and boundary-layer resolution
  • Interpretation of asymmetric flow fields
  • Use of dimensionless metrics for fair comparison
  • Engineering judgment in assessing model limitations
  • HPC-based simulation workflows
