Cornering Aerodynamics Analysis of a Formula SAE Race Car

Aerodynamic performance under straight-line and cornering flow conditions

Carnegie Mellon Racing · Formula SAE
Team research project · 2023
Conducted as part of the CMR aerodynamics effort

Context

Formula SAE race cars operate predominantly in cornering conditions, where vehicle performance depends strongly on aerodynamic downforce and drag. Despite this, aerodynamic analysis is often performed assuming straight-line flow, largely due to the simplicity of uniform inlet conditions and rectangular CFD domains.

This project was motivated by the question of how representative straight-line aerodynamic simulations are of real cornering performance. In particular, the goal was to quantify how aerodynamic forces change when the car experiences a velocity gradient across its width, as occurs during steady-state cornering. Understanding this sensitivity is important for interpreting CFD results correctly and for making informed aerodynamic design decisions.

Approach

The problem was framed as a comparative CFD study between straight-line flow and cornering flow conditions. Rather than attempting to directly compare absolute force values—which are affected by differences in local flow velocity—the analysis focused on relative aerodynamic performance metrics, particularly the downforce-to-drag ratio.

Two simulation domains were constructed:

  • A straight-line domain with uniform inlet velocity and symmetry conditions, serving as the baseline.
  • A cornering domain that introduces a lateral velocity gradient, simulating the curved flow field experienced during steady-state turning.

By running both domains across a range of velocities and comparing trends rather than isolated values, the study isolates the aerodynamic impact of cornering flow while maintaining numerical consistency.

Implementation & Tools

Geometry and Domain Setup

  • Simplified but aerodynamically representative CAD model including front and rear wings, chassis and driver body, and wheels
  • Two computational domains: rectangular straight-line domain and pie-shaped cornering domain

CFD Solver and Physics

  • Solver: ANSYS Fluent (pressure-based coupled solver)
  • Flow model: Incompressible RANS
  • Turbulence model: k–ω SST
  • Discretization: Second-order spatial schemes
  • Boundary conditions: moving ground, rotating wheels, velocity inlet with imposed lateral gradient (cornering case)

Mesh Strategy and Compute

  • Watertight geometry meshing workflow
  • Poly-hexcore volume mesh
  • Boundary layer resolution with first cell height ≈ 0.5 mm and y⁺ ≈ 30
  • Mesh sizes ranging from 8 million to 21 million cells
  • Simulations run on the Bridges-2 HPC cluster

Analysis & Insights

The results indicate that while cornering flow does degrade aerodynamic performance, the magnitude of the effect is modest. Downforce continues to scale predictably with velocity, and the aerodynamic package remains highly effective relative to the no-aero configuration.

Drag appears more sensitive to flow curvature than downforce, contributing to the observed reduction in the downforce-to-drag ratio. Importantly, the consistency of the reduction across speeds suggests that straight-line simulations remain useful for comparative design, provided their limitations are understood.

An additional outcome of this work is the development of a reusable cornering-flow CFD workflow that can be applied to future aerodynamic iterations.
